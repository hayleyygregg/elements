Nitrogen

Origin of the name: The name is derived from the Greek ‘nitron’ and ‘genes’ meaning nitre forming.

Nitrogen is the 5th most abundant element in the universe. 78% of Earth’s atmosphere is made of Nitrogen gas. Its compounds are present in organic materials, foods, fertilizers, explosives and poisons. It is crucial to life, but in excess it can also be harmful to the environment.

It is colourless and odourless both as gas and liquid and looks similar to water, according to Los Alamos. Liquid nitrogen is very cold and will immediately freeze skin on contact causing severe damage and frostbite. Nitrogen also plays an important role in DNA molecules.

History

Nitrogen was discovered in the 1760s by both Henry Cavendish and Joseph Priestley when they removed oxygen from air. They noted it extinguished a lighted candle and that a mouse breathing it would soon die. Neither man deduced that it was an element.

The first person to suggest this was a young student Daniel Rutherford in his doctorate thesis of September 1772 at Edinburgh, Scotland. In 1790 French chemist Antoine Laurent Lavoisier named the element azote, meaning "without life" because it seemed so chemically inactive.
