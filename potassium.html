Potassium

Origin of the name: The K symbol for the element comes from the Latin word ‘kalium’, which means potash in English (an alkaline potassium compound)

Potassium is a very reactive alkali metal and the seventh largest metal element found in the Earth's crust. Pure potassium is a soft, waxy metal that can be easily cut with a knife. It is never found by itself in nature, It is always bonded to other elements. When Potassium burns, a pale purple colored flame is produced. An example of this is putting Potassium into contact with water, which produces heat and hydrogen gas.

Potassium is essential to life, with ions found in most cells. Plant cells are particularly rich in potassium, which they get from the soil. Fertilizer contains potassium. The average human consumes up to 7 grams of potassium a day, and stores about 140 grams in the body cells. Instant coffee, sardines, nuts, raisins, potatoes and chocolate are foods that have above average potassium content.

History

Potassium was the first metal ever to be isolated. It had been discovered that chemical elements bond together to form compounds, and the bonding is electrical in nature. In 1807, British chemist Sir Humphry Davy started working to isolate elements. In his experiment he used electricity to split elements in chemical compounds.

He made solutions of potash and soda ash in water, and passed electric current through them, however, there were no results as the elements reacted with water. Once he discovered this he repeated the process without water, passing an electric current through molten potash, which led to the formation of tiny droplets of a metal. He placed the newly discovered metal in solutions including water, oil and hydrochloric acid. The metal reacted with water, exploding into a bright lilac flame, floated on oil and produced a red flame in hydrochloric acid. A few days later, Davy discovered sodium using the same method.
